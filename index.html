From your viewpoint (陈家乐 Chén Jiālè)

Same person, different name

Titles aren't properties of a person — they're properties of a relationship. Your 妈妈 (māma) is somebody's 婶婶 (shěnshen), somebody's 舅妈 (jiùmā), somebody's 小姑子 (xiǎogūzi) and somebody's 儿媳 (érxí), all at the same dinner table. Tap "see the tree as…" on anyone to feel this.

堂 táng vs 表 biǎo — follow the surname

Only your father's brothers' children are 堂 (táng) cousins — notice they're the only cousins surnamed 陈 (Chén) like you. Everyone else — 姑姑 (gūgu)'s daughter (a 吴 Wú!) and all the cousins on your mum's side — is a 表 (biǎo) cousin.

Number them by seniority

With several uncles or aunts of the same type, rank them by age: 大伯、二伯、三叔 (dàbó, èrbó, sānshū), 大舅、二舅 (dàjiù, èrjiù). The eldest is 大 (dà), then 二、三、四 (èr, sān, sì)…

Saying it to their face vs. about them

Terms like 岳父 (yuèfù), 大伯子 (dàbǎizi) or 儿媳 (érxí) are how you refer to someone (背称 bèichēng). Face to face, parents-in-law are 爸爸 / 妈妈 (bàba / māma), siblings-in-law get a name or 哥 / 姐 (gē / jiě), and elders simply call juniors by name.

Dialects layer on top — and vary by family

The Hokkien, Cantonese and Teochew columns show common Singapore/Malaysia usage, but every family has its own habits. The Hainanese column is left blank on purpose — reliable written sources are scarce and family variation is huge, so ask your elders and fill in the DIALECTS table at the top of this file.
